# Mode B — Interviewer Design

Designing or preparing to run an interview. The core moves: job analysis → competencies → behavioral/situational questions → behaviorally-anchored rubric → calibrated panel debrief.

Copy this checklist:

```
Interview Design Progress:
- [ ] Step 1: Job analysis → 3–5 core competencies
- [ ] Step 2: Question bank (behavioral + situational, 2–3 per competency)
- [ ] Step 3: Behaviorally-anchored rubric (what 1, 3, 5 look like)
- [ ] Step 4: Loop structure (who covers what, no overlap)
- [ ] Step 5: Bias and calibration check
- [ ] Step 6: Debrief protocol (independent score → discussion)
- [ ] Step 7: Candidate experience + cross-cultural calibration (only if relevant)
- [ ] Highest-leverage move before the first interview
```

## 1. Job analysis

Identify 3–5 competencies the role actually requires. Cap at 5 — trying to score 8 competencies in a 60-minute interview produces shallow signal on each. For each competency, write a one-sentence operational definition: what does this look like in the actual work, not as an HR adjective.

Distinguish must-haves from nice-to-haves. The interview should over-weight must-haves.

## 2. Question bank

For each competency, write 2–3 questions, mixing:
- **Behavioral** ("tell me about a time when…") — past behavior as predictor. Stronger for senior candidates with more past data.
- **Situational** ("how would you approach…") — stated reasoning. Stronger for junior or career-change candidates.

Every candidate gets the same core questions in the same order. Probes ("what specifically did you do?", "what was the outcome?") are adaptive on top of the fixed spine.

Avoid: trick questions, brain teasers without job relevance, anything that tests for shared cultural reference (these have high adverse-impact risk and low predictive validity).

## 3. Behaviorally-anchored rubric

For each competency, define what a 1, 3, and 5 look like in observable behavior — not adjectives. Bad: "5 = excellent communicator." Good: "5 = unprompted, articulates trade-offs between options with concrete consequences; adjusts depth based on listener; surfaces what they don't know." Without behavioral anchors, structured questions still produce unstructured ratings.

Some teams deliberately exclude a "hire / no hire" field from the rubric — collect evidence-anchored scores per competency, let the hiring manager combine. This separates evidence-gathering from the decision and reduces post-hoc rationalization.

## 4. Loop structure

Multiple interviewers should cover *different* competencies — overlap wastes candidate time and produces redundant rather than independent signal. Map the matrix: which interviewer covers which competencies, and what is each interviewer specifically *not* responsible for.

Build in candidate-experience time: 5 minutes for the candidate to ask questions, brief context on next steps. Bad candidate experience tanks acceptance rates among the candidates you most want.

## 5. Bias and calibration check

Name the highest-risk biases for this loop:
- **Affinity bias**: scoring up candidates who feel like the panel. The most common signal: "culture fit" as a scoring dimension. Replace with values defined as observable behaviors, or drop the dimension.
- **Halo / horn**: one strong (or weak) impression colors all scores. Mitigate by scoring per-competency and per-question, not overall.
- **Recency / primacy**: candidates earlier or later in a batch score systematically differently. Mitigate by interviewing across multiple days and re-checking earlier scores.
- **First-strong-opinion conformity in debrief** — see step 6.

Run a calibration session before the loop: panel scores the same recorded candidate response against the rubric, debates discrepancies, anchors on behavioral evidence. Skipping this is the single biggest reason "structured" interviews still produce unstructured scoring.

## 6. Debrief protocol

The order matters:
- Each interviewer submits independent rubric scores **before** discussion. Conformity bias is strong; whoever speaks first anchors the panel.
- Discussion debates discrepancies by pointing to specific behavioral evidence ("I scored 2 on ownership because they said 'we did' three times without specifying their part") — not gut.
- A 5-vs-2 split is information, not noise. Investigate it.

## 7. Candidate experience + cross-cultural calibration (only if relevant)

**Candidate experience.** For competitive candidates, the interview is also a *recruiting* moment. Specific things that move the needle on acceptance when you make an offer:
- The candidate gets to ask substantive questions to someone senior — ideally the person who would be their manager — for at least 10 minutes.
- Someone explicitly sells the role: what's exciting, what's hard, what growth looks like. Don't assume the JD has done this.
- Loop runs on time, with clear next steps communicated within 48 hours regardless of outcome. Silence kills offers more than rejection does.
- Rejection includes one piece of specific, kindly-delivered feedback if the candidate asks. This is reputational gold for the company over time.

**Cross-cultural calibration of the panel.** If the candidate pool is cross-cultural (e.g., Western interviewers evaluating East Asian, South Asian, Middle Eastern, or African candidates — or vice versa), the rubric needs to not penalize non-default communication norms:
- "Demonstrates ownership" should be defined by *content* (specific decisions made, trade-offs articulated), not by *style* (enthusiasm, self-promotion language). A calm candidate who says "the team decided X and I implemented Y, then noticed Z and changed approach" demonstrates ownership; an excited candidate who says "I led the whole thing!" without specifics does not.
- Silence between question and answer should not be scored down — many candidates from high-context cultures pause longer to compose answers, and it correlates with thoughtfulness, not weakness.
- Probing should be calibrated to the candidate, not the panel: candidates who use less self-promoting language often need more direct probing ("what specifically did *you* do?") to surface evidence the rubric needs. This is the panel's job, not a candidate failure.
