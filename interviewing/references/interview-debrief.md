# Mode C — Interview Debrief

After the interview, the goal is learning, not justification. Same scaffold (competency → behavioral evidence → calibrated judgment), applied retrospectively.

Copy this checklist:

```
Debrief Progress:
- [ ] Step 1: Hygiene (thank-you for candidate; logistics for interviewer)
- [ ] Step 2: Diagnose what actually happened (specific moments, not vibes)
- [ ] Step 3: Distinguish performance issues from process/luck issues
- [ ] Step 4: One thing to do differently next time
- [ ] Highest-leverage move in the next 24 hours
```

## 1. Hygiene

**Candidate side**: Send a thank-you within 24 hours. Individual notes to each interviewer, each referencing something specific from that conversation. Templated thank-yous read as templates and add no value. Do this even if the interview felt mid — it's reputational and sometimes flips a borderline call.

**Interviewer side**: Submit independent rubric scores before any panel discussion (see `interviewer-design.md` step 6). Communicate next steps to the candidate within 48 hours regardless of outcome.

## 2. Diagnose what actually happened

Pick the 2–3 specific moments that mattered. Not "the interview went OK overall" — actual moments: the question that was wobbly, the answer that landed, the silence that felt long.

For the **candidate**: which questions felt strong, which felt weak, what question caught you off-guard that you should add to your prep bank for next time.

For the **interviewer / panel**: where did the rubric give you signal, where did it fall flat, was there a competency you couldn't get evidence on, did any question reliably differentiate candidates or did everyone score the same.

## 3. Distinguish performance from process / luck

Self-rated interview performance correlates poorly with outcome. Before drawing conclusions, separate:
- **Performance issues** (real, fixable): rambling, missing a competency story, weak "why this role" answer, didn't ask good questions.
- **Process issues** (signal about the loop, not you): rushed timing, unclear question, interviewer who seemed checked out, panel that contradicted each other.
- **Luck** (information-free): interviewer mood, time of day, whether your story happened to match a thing they cared about.

For interviewers, the equivalent split: distinguish "this candidate is below bar" from "this candidate is below my preferred profile" — the second is bias-shaped, not evidence-shaped.

## 4. One thing to do differently next time

Specific. Not "be more concise" — "cut the situation in the conflict story to 2 sentences and time it." Not "be a better interviewer" — "calibrate the 'ownership' rubric anchor with the panel before the next loop."

If the answer is "nothing, the interview went well" — fine, but check it against step 2. If you can't name a specific moment that mattered, you probably haven't debriefed yet, just rationalized.

## Things to actively *not* do

- **Candidate**: don't follow up before the date they gave you. Hiring loops have many failure modes unrelated to you (req frozen, internal candidate, headcount cut). Reading your performance from silence isn't useful.
- **Candidate**: don't decide you bombed it based on vibes. The data is the outcome, not the feeling.
- **Interviewer**: don't rationalize a hire/no-hire decision after the fact to match the panel consensus. If your rubric scores disagree with the consensus and you have evidence, that's information.
