# Mode A — Interviewee Prep

The backbone is **STAR** (Situation, Task, Action, Result) story bank, mapped against role-specific competencies. For multi-round loops, run this mode before each round — different rounds emphasize different competencies (recruiter screen = fit + logistics, hiring manager = role specifics, panel = deep skills, leadership = strategy + culture).

Copy this checklist and check off each step as you complete it:

```
Interview Prep Progress:
- [ ] Step 1: Decode the role (competencies the interviewer is actually scoring)
- [ ] Step 2: Audit the story bank (3–5 STAR stories mapped to competencies)
- [ ] Step 3: Universals (tell-me-about-yourself, why-this-role, weaknesses, questions for them)
- [ ] Step 4: Role-specific layer (technical / case / portfolio / live exercise)
- [ ] Step 5: Cross-cultural / format calibration (only if relevant)
- [ ] Step 6: Failure-mode pre-mortem
- [ ] Highest-leverage move in next 24 hours
```

## 1. Decode the role

From the job description (and whatever the user knows about the team), extract the 3–5 competencies the interviewer is most likely scoring. Distinguish:
- **Threshold competencies** — must clear the bar, but won't differentiate
- **Differentiator competencies** — what tips the decision

Most candidates over-prepare for threshold and under-prepare for differentiator. Name both explicitly.

## 2. Audit the story bank

The candidate needs 3–5 STAR stories that, combined, cover all the competencies. For each:
- **Situation**: 2–3 sentences of context (date, scope, stakes)
- **Task**: their specific responsibility (1–2 sentences)
- **Action**: what *they* personally did — this should be ~50% of the response on delivery. Catch "we" language; replace with "I" where the user was actually the actor
- **Result**: quantified where possible. If qualitative, name the observable evidence (feedback received, decision changed, system shipped)

For each story, list which competencies it can serve. Look for the gap: any competency with no story is the highest-priority thing to fix before the interview.

If a story sounds memorized when said out loud, that is a problem to flag — varied phrasing across practice runs beats a polished script that breaks under follow-up probing.

## 3. Universals

Every interview will probably ask some version of:
- **Tell me about yourself** — ~90 seconds, present → past → future arc, ending with why this role specifically. Not a chronological resume read.
- **Why this role / company** — must reference something specific that could not be said about a competitor. "I love your mission" applied to any company is a soft fail.
- **Weakness / failure** — pick a real one, name what was learned, name what changed. "I'm a perfectionist" is the canonical bad answer.
- **Salary expectations** — when asked ("what's your range?"), have a number based on actual market research (Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, local equivalents, recruiter conversations) — not a wish. Bias slightly high but be ready to defend it. If asked early in the process, it's fair to ask back: "what range have you budgeted for this role?" first. Real comp negotiation happens once an offer is on the table; for that, see `/negotiating` (when available).
- **Questions for us** — prepare 5–8 (some will get answered during the interview, leaving you with too few). Mix levels: role/team, strategy, culture, "what does success look like at 6 months". Never lead with comp.

If any of these isn't ready, draft it together with the user.

## 4. Role-specific layer

**By role family** (pick what applies):
- **Technical (eng, data, ML)**: live coding or system design — practice *out loud* under time pressure with someone watching. The performance gap between silent solo practice and observed practice is large. Note the loop's language/tools if known.
- **Case (consulting, PM, strategy)**: framework + adaptable problem decomposition. Practice with cases that *don't* match textbook frameworks — interviewers probe for adaptability.
- **Portfolio (design, research, applied ML)**: 2–3 deep dives, each with the *decision* layer (why this approach over alternatives), not just what was built. Anticipate "why didn't you do X instead?"
- **Sales / commercial**: a pitch component is likely. Have a 2-minute pitch for *yourself* and one for the company's product as you understand it.

**By interview format** (more than one usually applies):
- **Live coding / whiteboard**: think out loud always, even when stuck. Silence is the failure mode. State assumptions before coding.
- **Take-home assignment**: time-box your effort honestly (3 hours doesn't mean 12). Include a brief README on what you'd do with more time. The README often matters more than the code itself for senior roles.
- **Panel**: address the asker, sweep eye contact across the panel. Note who hasn't spoken — they're often scoring hardest.
- **Group / cohort interview** (rarer, mostly entry-level / consulting): contribute substantively, don't dominate, surface other candidates' good points. Trying to win the room loses you the interview.
- **Presentation interview**: the Q&A matters more than the slides. Plan to finish 5 minutes early. Anticipate the three hardest questions.

## 5. Cross-cultural and remote calibration (only if relevant)

**Cross-cultural** — if the interviewer's culture is meaningfully different from the candidate's defaults, calibrate. The most common asymmetries:
- **Self-presentation**: Western (esp. US) defaults to enthusiasm and explicit self-promotion ("I'm passionate about", "I led the team to"). East Asian and many high-context cultures default to calmer, more deferential framing with obstacles acknowledged. Cross-culture mismatches: a calm Thai/Japanese/Korean candidate can read as low-energy to a US interviewer; a high-energy US candidate can read as boastful to a Japanese interviewer. The fix is *not* to fully adopt the other side's norm — it is to dial 30% in their direction while staying authentic.
- **Power distance**: high-PD context (much of Asia, Middle East, Eastern/Southern Europe) — formal address (titles, "Khun", "san"), more deferential posture, wait-for-prompt rhythm. Low-PD (US, Australia, Canada, Nordics) — first names, more candidate-driven conversation, expected to push back when you disagree.
- **Eye contact, silence, voice**: sustained Western-style eye contact reads as confidence in some places, as aggressive in others. Silence reads as thoughtfulness in much of East Asia, as awkward in the West. Calibrate.

For multinationals, research the *company's* dominant interview culture — many impose Western-style norms regardless of country.

**Remote / video specifics** (common for senior interviews):
- **Tech check the day before** — camera angle (eye-level, not nostril view), lighting (window in front, not behind), audio (test on the platform you'll use), backup plan if the connection drops (have the interviewer's phone number or recruiter contact ready).
- **Eye contact = camera, not screen.** Counterintuitive but it's how the interviewer perceives "looking at them." Practice this — it feels weird.
- **Energy compensation**: video flattens affect by ~20%. Slightly more animation than feels natural reads as normal on the other end. Don't overdo it — *slightly*.
- **Live coding remote**: confirm the platform (CoderPad, HackerRank, shared doc) before the interview. Know how to share your screen on it. Have a second monitor or tab open with notes if allowed.
- **Phone-only screens**: voice modulation matters more without visual affect. Stand up while talking — it changes the voice. Quiet environment.

## 6. Failure-mode pre-mortem

Imagine the interview goes badly. What's the single most likely reason?
- "I'll freeze on a behavioral I didn't prep" → drill the gap from step 2
- "I'll over-explain a weakness" → tighten the wording now
- "I won't have a smart question for the hiring manager" → research the team
- "I'll bomb the live coding" → do one timed practice tonight

If the answer to "what would tank this" is concrete, it should drive the next 24 hours of prep.
